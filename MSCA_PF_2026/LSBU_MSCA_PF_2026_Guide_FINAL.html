CRITICAL RULES

Read this page before anything else. These are the four rules most likely to result in an inadmissible or unfunded proposal. They are not negotiable, not subject to appeal, and not something RAD can fix after the fact.

CRITICAL RULE 1 — MOBILITY WINDOW

You must not have resided or carried out your main activity in the UK for more than 12 months in the 36 months immediately before the deadline. The mobility window runs from 10 September 2023 to 9 September 2026. Conference visits and short stays do not count if they were not your main activity. If you are close to the limit, contact RAD before drafting anything else. Breach of this rule = inadmissible proposal.

CRITICAL RULE 2 — 80% RESUBMISSION BLOCK

If you submitted to the 2025 call with the same beneficiary (European Fellowship) or the same beneficiary and outgoing host (Global Fellowship) and scored below 80%, you cannot resubmit to this call with those same organisations. Scored 80% or above: eligible — mark in Part A, do not reference in Part B. Application declared ineligible or inadmissible: the block does not apply. Submitted to the 2024 or earlier call: the block does not apply.

CRITICAL RULE 3 — 10-PAGE HARD LIMIT (PART B1)

Sections 1, 2, and 3 of Part B1 together must not exceed 10 pages. The submission system enforces this automatically: anything beyond page 10 is made invisible after the deadline and will not be evaluated. There is no appeal. Do not attempt to circumvent this by using reduced font sizes or compressed line spacing. The minimum font size is 11pt; the minimum line spacing is single-line.

CRITICAL RULE 4 — 70% THRESHOLD PER CRITERION

Excellence, Impact, and Implementation are each scored out of 100 points by at least three independent experts. Each criterion must reach a minimum of 70 points. A proposal scoring 95, 95, and 65 is not funded, regardless of its average. There is no way to trade a strong section against a weak one. A proposal that neglects Implementation because the researcher "doesn't have time" will fail.

You must hold a doctoral degree or have unconditionally defended your thesis before the application deadline of 9 September 2026. The defence must be unconditional: no further requirements or corrections that need to be addressed. Supporting documentation may be requested.

2.7 Worked Eligibility Scenarios

Scenario A: UK-based researcher applying for European Fellowship at LSBU

Dr Amara Diallo completed her PhD in Dakar in 2019 and has been based at the University of Edinburgh since October 2023. She wants to apply for a European Fellowship at LSBU. Her UK stay runs from October 2023 to September 2026 — approximately 35 months. This exceeds the 12-month limit. She is ineligible for a European Fellowship at LSBU. She could apply for a European Fellowship at an institution in another EU Member State or Associated Country where she has not been based.

Scenario B: Researcher close to the 8-year limit

Dr Yuki Tanaka was awarded her PhD in March 2018. By September 2026 she will have 8 years and 6 months of calendar time since her doctorate. However, she took 14 months of maternity leave in 2020-2021 and 6 months of parental leave in 2023. With these deductions, her net FTE research experience is 7 years and 10 months. She is eligible, but should use the REA Self-Assessment Tool and gather all documentary evidence before submitting.

Scenario C: Resubmission from 2025 call

Dr Carlos Mendes submitted a European Fellowship application to LSBU in 2025 and scored 78%. He cannot resubmit to LSBU in 2026. He could, however, submit to a different UK institution (e.g., King's College London) as the beneficiary, provided he meets the mobility rule for that institution's country.

Scenario D: Global Fellowship applicant

Dr Priya Sharma is an Indian national who has been a long-term resident of the UK for the past 7 years. She wants to spend 18 months at MIT (USA) and then return to LSBU for 12 months. She is eligible for a Global Fellowship: she is a long-term resident of a Horizon Europe Associated Country (UK), MIT is in a non-associated Third Country (USA), and LSBU would be her return-phase host. She must obtain a signed letter of commitment from MIT before submission.

3. The LSBU Context

3.1 Why LSBU as Host

LSBU sits at Elephant & Castle, embedded in Southwark's innovation ecosystem. The 50% secondment rule makes this geography materially valuable: Guy's and St Thomas' NHS Foundation Trust, King's College Hospital, Southwark Council, and the Greater London Authority are all within commutable range. Do not describe this as a generic urban advantage. Name the specific partner, the specific asset they provide, and the specific research objective it serves.

LSBU has a strong track record in Knowledge Transfer Partnerships (KTP), consistently ranking among the UK's most active KTP providers. This is proven institutional capacity for the intersectoral secondment structure that the 2026 call now permits at greater scale. If relevant, frame Southwark as a living lab for place-based research on health inequalities, urban sustainability, and social mobility. Evaluators recognise proximity to policy and practice partners as a genuine asset when the proposal demonstrates how it will be used.

Do not write generic institutional boilerplate. The following are defensible, specific claims for LSBU host capacity statements:

Writing Tips

  • Use a memorable acronym: this is how your proposal will be referred to in the reviewer panel's discussion. You cannot change it after submission. Test it for unintended meanings in other languages.
  • Keywords determine evaluator assignment. Choose with strategic intent across your interdisciplinary scope. The first two descriptors must be linked to your selected panel; others can come from other panels.
  • Avoid jargon. Explain abbreviations once. Use simple, clear text. Non-specialists may read this.
  • Be consistent with terminology throughout. The first person (I/me) or third person (the researcher) are both acceptable. Avoid first-person plural (we): this is your individual fellowship.
  • Do not copy-paste from other documents. Every sentence should be written for this proposal.
  • Ensure diagrams are legible when printed in black and white.

4.2 Part A: Administrative Forms

Part A is not a formality. It is an integral part of your proposal. Common oversights:

  • Acronym: choose carefully. You cannot change it. Test it for unintended meanings in other languages.
  • Keywords: these select your evaluators. Choose strategically across your interdisciplinary scope. First 3 descriptors are mandatory; first 2 must be linked to your selected panel.
  • Panel: select the evaluation panel that best matches your research (CHE, ECO, ENG, ENV, LIF, MAT, PHY, SOC).
  • Publications (Section 4): list up to 5 publications for the host organisation or department — the supervisor's key papers, not yours. Do not confuse this with your own publication record in the CV.
  • Previous projects: list relevant ongoing or completed projects demonstrating institutional capacity.
  • Budget table: ensure all durations are correct. Wrong durations are the single most common budget error.
  • Ensure exact consistency between Part A and Part B on dates, institutions, and project details.

4.3 The Abstract

Maximum 2,000 characters including spaces. Write it for evaluators, not for a specialist journal. Keep it accessible, persuasive, and jargon-free.

Annotated Abstract Template

[Area] is a critical challenge because [specific problem, with evidence]... (Context: 1 sentence)

This fellowship aims to [O1] and [O2] using [approach] at LSBU, hosted by [supervisor/centre]... (Objectives: 1–2 sentences)

The project combines [A] with [B] to [aim], including a [duration] secondment at [host] to access [specific asset]... (Methodology & Implementation: 2 sentences)

The results will inform [specific target group], contributing to [specific advancement], while enhancing the fellow's career by developing [specific skill] and positioning them for [career goal]... (Impact: 1–2 sentences)

Abstract Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting with a definition of your field. The evaluator knows what bioinformatics is.
  • Using all 2,000 characters. Brevity signals clarity of thought.
  • Mentioning your career development plan. The abstract is about the research, not your aspirations.
  • Including citations. The abstract is not a literature review.
  • Using acronyms without expansion. Non-specialists may read this.

5. Part B1: Excellence (50% Weighting)

Section 1 is where you prove you are a serious researcher with a genuinely important question. It carries the heaviest intellectual weight of the proposal. It is also where the most common failure mode occurs: impressive-sounding prose that does not answer the evaluator's actual question.

What the Evaluator Actually Thinks

"I read hundreds of proposals each year. Those that score above 90% do not begin with grand claims about the European Research Area. They begin with a precise, evidence-based statement of what the applicant will do, why it matters now, and why they are the best person to do it. The state of the art is not a literature review. It is a structured argument for why this project is necessary."

5.1 Research Objectives & State of the Art

Quality and Pertinence of Objectives

Your objectives must be measurable, verifiable, and realistically achievable. Name the policy framework, health burden, emissions target, or industrial gap you are addressing. Be specific about why this matters now.

Number your objectives O1, O2, O3 and map each to a research work package in brackets (e.g., O1 [WP1]). Evaluators should be able to trace every objective through to a specific task in your Gantt chart.

✗ Weak Formulation

"This project will revolutionise our understanding of urban heat islands using novel data approaches."

Evaluator: Hyperbolic, unmeasurable, vague. What does "revolutionise" mean? What data? What approach?

✓ Strong Formulation

"Objective 1 [WP1]: To quantify micro-climate variance across three Southwark housing estates by deploying a 50-node IoT sensor network over 12 months, producing a validated dataset for urban heat-island modelling."

Evaluator: Specific, measurable, linked to a WP, time-bounded.

Going Beyond the State of the Art

Break the state of the art into short paragraphs, one per research objective. For each:

  1. Outline the current knowledge level with up-to-date references (2023–2026 should dominate).
  2. Identify the specific gap, limitation, or unanswered question.
  3. State how your project addresses it — methodologically distinct, evidence-filling, or building on your prior work.
  4. Finish with a bold statement of exactly how the project advances the field.

Prohibited formulations: do not use hyperbolic adjectives to describe your work. Instead, write that it is methodologically distinct, addresses an evidence gap, or builds upon your prior work on a specific problem.

5.2 Methodology, Gender & Open Science

Overall Methodology

Describe and explain your overall methodology, including concepts, models, and assumptions. Be specific about experiments, techniques, and equipment. If secondments or placements are included, justify exactly why: use of equipment, access to data, acquisition of techniques, or exposure to policy or industry practice.

You do not have space to describe everything in detail, but you must give enough to show how the research will be conducted. Use graphics where appropriate — but ensure they are legible when printed in black and white.

Integration of Methods and Disciplines

Explain how expertise from different disciplines will be brought together. If interdisciplinarity is unnecessary, provide a justification. Listing disciplines is not interdisciplinarity. Show how a technique or dataset from one discipline generates a hypothesis, question, or analytical constraint that the other discipline is equipped to test or resolve. If you cannot describe that exchange in one sentence, the interdisciplinarity is not yet real.

Gender Dimension and Diversity

The question is not "does my research involve people?" It is: "would my findings change, or my methods require modification, if I disaggregated by sex or gender?" Use this four-question framework:

  1. Does sex or gender shape the phenomenon you are studying? In health, behaviour, social, and language research the answer is almost always yes. In some computational, materials, or physical science contexts it may be no, but state why explicitly.
  2. Could your data, sample, or model carry a hidden sex/gender bias? Training datasets, clinical cohorts, and historical records frequently over-represent one sex. If you are using such sources, state what the bias is and how you will address it.
  3. Does your methodology produce differentiated outputs by sex or gender, or does it aggregate by default? Aggregation is not neutral: it reproduces existing inequalities. If you aggregate, justify it.
  4. Do your results carry different implications for different groups? A drug dosage study or a language model trained on gendered corpora will have asymmetric impacts. Name them.

If questions 1–4 all return no, write a short, argued justification. Do not write "gender is not relevant to this research." Write: "This study measures [X] in [material/system/process]. The phenomenon operates at [level] and does not vary by the biological sex of any participant, nor are gender norms embedded in the analytical framework, for the following reason: [reason]." That is a strong justification. "Gender is not relevant" is not.

Worked Example — Gender Dimension

Researcher A is developing a machine learning model to predict hospital readmission rates from electronic health records. The initial inclination is to treat gender as irrelevant because the model is computational, not clinical. This is wrong. Readmission rates differ by sex due to differences in symptom presentation, pain reporting behaviour, and discharge decision-making by clinical staff. The training data are drawn from a hospital cohort in which certain conditions (e.g., cardiac events) were historically under-diagnosed in women. The model will inherit this bias unless corrected. The researcher commits to training and validating separate submodels by sex and reporting disaggregated performance metrics. This scores well because it is specific, evidenced, and integrated into the research design.

Researcher B is working on the corrosion resistance of a novel aluminium alloy at high temperature. Questions 1–4 return no: the physical and chemical processes are not sex- or gender-differentiated, no human participants are involved, and no differential social impact is plausible. Researcher B writes a four-sentence justification explaining exactly this. This also scores well, because it is argued rather than absent.

The section that scores poorly is the one that writes "we acknowledge the importance of gender equality" and stops there. That is not a justification. It is a sentence that signals the researcher has not thought about the question.

Open Science Practices

You have approximately half a page for open science, including research data management. Make them count.

  • State which repository you will use for publications, data, and outputs. LSBU uses Pure (Elsevier RIMS) as its institutional repository.
  • Name the licence (CC BY, CC BY-NC, etc.).
  • Include a Data Management Plan (DMP) as a mandatory deliverable by month 6.
  • State your approach to FAIR principles for data, models, and papers.
  • Mention reproducibility measures: code repositories, workflow documentation, pre-registration if appropriate.
Common Failure — Vague Open Science Statement

"We will make our data available in accordance with FAIR principles." This scores 60%. A strong statement names the repository (LSBU Pure), the licence (CC BY), the timeline (DMP by month 6, data deposited on publication), and the specific challenge (e.g., patient data requires anonymisation under GDPR before deposition).

5.3 Supervision & Training

Supervisor Qualifications

Provide the supervisor's key achievements: years in the field, awards, international collaborations, coordinated projects, publications in key journals, H-index, patents, and number of supervised researchers. Include a success story if one exists. Are their former supervisees now in leading positions?

If including a co-supervisor, explain their added value. Roles must be clearly defined and complementary. Refer explicitly to the MSCA Guidelines on Supervision and the 2023 European Charter for Researchers (Pillar 4). State that you will adhere to them.

Supervisor/Host Note — Supervisor Obligations

By agreeing to supervise an MSCA PF fellow, you commit to: developing a Career Development Plan (CDP) with the fellow by month 6; providing regular, structured feedback; supporting the fellow's access to training and networking; adhering to the MSCA Guidelines on Supervision; and supporting the fellow's career development beyond the fellowship. These are not aspirational commitments. They are grant conditions.

Planned Training Activities

Identify skill gaps and match them to specific training opportunities. The best training plans connect demonstrated gaps to concrete solutions:

  • Scientific skills: new techniques and methods, acquired through research or specific courses. Name the courses and the institution offering them.
  • Transferable skills: teaching, project management, proposal writing, international collaboration, IP, gender awareness. Name the specific training programme at LSBU or elsewhere.
  • Career Development Plan: becomes a mandatory deliverable by month 6. Describe your needs and career goals. Be honest about gaps.

This subsection asks you to quantify and contextualise the importance of your project's contribution to scientific, societal, and economic impacts. The Honest PI Standard requires four components for every impact assertion:

Proposals that meet all four components score higher on credibility. Proposals that omit component 4 are read as naive.

7. Part B1: Implementation (20% Weighting)

Section 3 is where you prove you can actually do this in 24 months with the resources available. It is assessed for realism, not ambition. A proposal that is intellectually brilliant but operationally incoherent will not score 70%.

8. Part B2: Supporting Sections

Part B2 contains the practical and administrative material that supports your scientific case. Do not treat it as an afterthought. Inconsistencies between Part A and Part B2 are red flags for evaluators.

8.1 CV, Publications & Research Experience

Indicative length: 5 pages. Structure your CV to make the evaluator's job easy:

  • Education: reverse chronological. Include the exact date of doctoral degree award (must match Part A exactly, in dd/mm/yyyy format).
  • Research experience: reverse chronological. Dates, institution, one-line description of responsibilities.
  • Publications: reverse chronological. Highlight first-author and corresponding-author papers. Include DOIs. Provide a brief qualitative assessment of significance — do not use Journal Impact Factor.
  • Presentations: selected invited talks and conference presentations.
  • Awards and funding: competitive fellowships, prizes, grants.
  • Other: languages, technical skills, professional memberships, non-academic experience.
What the Evaluator Actually Thinks — CV Consistency

"The CV is read quickly but carefully. I look for consistency: if Part A says the doctorate was awarded in March 2020 and the CV says March 2021, that is a red flag. If the publication list in Part A (the host's papers) does not match the CV (the researcher's papers), I note it. These are not the same list. Confusing them is a common error."

Applicant Action — CV Consistency Check

Check your CV against Part A right now. Is the date of doctorate award identical in both (dd/mm/yyyy)? Are the 5 publications in Part A the supervisor's papers, not yours? Is your own publication record in the CV complete and consistent? If not, fix it before you read another word.

List up to 5 publications in Part A (host organisation/supervisor's papers, not yours). Include your full publication record in the CV. Every publication should be deposited in LSBU Pure with open access where possible. If a publication is not yet open access, state the plan and timeline.

8.4 Ethics, Security & Environment

Ethics Information (Part B2 Section 6)

If your research involves activities raising ethics issues, complete this section. Even if you believe there are no issues, state this explicitly and explain why. Common issues requiring scrutiny:

  • Human participants (interviews, surveys, experiments, observation).
  • Human cells, tissues, or personal data (health records, genetic data, biometric data).
  • Animals (vertebrates and cephalopods).
  • Research in non-EU countries (dual-use technologies, indigenous communities).
  • Security concerns (pathogens, toxins, sensitive dual-use technologies).
  • Children, vulnerable adults, or prisoners.
Ethics Timing Warning

LSBU School-level ethics approval takes 8–12 weeks. NHS REC/HRA approval takes 3–6 months. If your project requires ethics approval, factor this into your Gantt chart. Do not assume approval will be granted before the project starts. Contact RAD at papa@lsbu.ac.uk for guidance on the appropriate committee.

Evaluation Process & Results

Your proposal will be assigned to at least 3 independent experts (up to 5) based on your chosen descriptors and keywords. Each expert writes an Individual Evaluation Report. All assigned experts then meet for a consensus discussion and draft a Consensus Report / Evaluation Summary Report (ESR). The ESR is the document you will receive if your proposal is evaluated.

If your proposal scores 85% or above but is not funded due to budget constraints, you will receive the MSCA Seal of Excellence — a quality label that can help you attract other funders. Specific support schemes exist for Seal of Excellence holders.

Resubmission Strategy

If your proposal is not funded:

  • Read the ESR carefully. Identify the specific weaknesses named by the evaluators.
  • If you scored ≥ 80%, you can resubmit to the next call with the same beneficiary. Mark the resubmission in Part A. Do not reference the previous submission in Part B.
  • If you scored < 80%, you must change the beneficiary (EF) or beneficiary + outgoing host (GF) to be eligible for the next call.
  • Contact RAD to discuss your ESR and plan your resubmission strategy.

Verification Outcomes

ItemOriginal Draft StatusVerified OutcomeAction Taken
A.1 — Mandatory Impact Tables Unverified. Draft noted that Section 2.3 may be optional in general HE templates but MSCA PF uses its own template. Resolved. The MSCA PF 2026 Part B template (Version 8.0) includes three subsections under Impact: 2.1 (career perspectives), 2.2 (dissemination/exploitation), and 2.3 (magnitude of contribution). All three are required. The RADIANCE handbook confirms structured KPI tables are expected for dissemination activities. Guide updated to include all three Impact subsections with specific guidance and templates.
A.2 — Refugee Status Treatment Unverified. Draft noted refugee status is not listed as a distinct FTE deduction. Verified. Refugee status provides mobility-rule flexibility (time spent waiting for status under the Geneva Convention does not count toward the 12-month mobility limit). It is NOT a distinct deduction category for the 8-year FTE research experience limit. Applicants must use standard deduction categories. Guide updated with precise, sourced statement. Critical rule callout added.
A.3 — LSBU KTP "Top-Three UK Ranking" Partially verified. No current authoritative league table confirmed a specific ranking. Resolved. No current authoritative league table could be located to confirm a specific numerical ranking. The claim has been downgraded. Claim downgraded to "consistently ranking among the UK's most active KTP providers" throughout the guide.
A.4 — Part B Template Version Not yet available at time of original drafting. Verified. The 2026 MSCA PF Part B template is available on the F&T Portal. The guide now aligns with the current template structure (Excellence 1.1/1.2/1.3, Impact 2.1/2.2/2.3, Implementation 3.1/3.2) and confirms the strict B2 table length limits. All template references updated. B2 table limits (1 page beneficiary; ½ page partners) confirmed and highlighted.
Unit Costs & UK CCC Draft stated UK CCC as 143.5% (from 2026-2027 WP). Verified. UK CCC for 2026-2027 is confirmed at 143.5%. Base living allowance EUR 6,350/month. Adjusted UK rate: EUR 9,112/month. All other unit costs verified. Financial table updated with precise verified figures. Common failure callout added for wrong CCC.
Call Deadline 9 September 2026, 17:00 Brussels time. Verified. Confirmed by UKRO, REA presentation, and F&T Portal. No change required.
Budget EUR 399.05 million. Verified. EUR 339.19m European; EUR 59.86m Global. Confirmed by REA presentation and DG EAC presentation. Budget breakdown added to call facts table.
Success Rate Not in original draft. Verified. 2025 call: ~9.6% success rate; over 17,000 proposals for ~1,600 funded projects. Added to call facts table for context.
